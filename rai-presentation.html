Data Sources, Potential Bias & Mitigations

AI Use Case Data Sources Potential Sources of Bias Mitigation Strategies
Predictive Readmission Electronic Health Records (EHR), insurance claims, socioeconomic data Underrepresentation of uninsured or rural patients; historical disparities in care documentation Augment datasets with community health data; stratified sampling; regular demographic audits
Diagnostic Imaging Hospital radiology archives, public medical imaging datasets (e.g., CheXpert) Training images skewed toward certain demographics; equipment variation across facilities Curate diverse imaging datasets across age, ethnicity, and sex; normalize for imaging hardware differences
Patient Triage Chatbot Patient-reported symptoms, prior call-center logs, clinical literature Language and literacy bias; cultural differences in symptom reporting; lack of multilingual data Include multilingual training data; user-test with diverse populations; use plain-language design
Treatment Recommendation Clinical trial results, patient histories, pharmacological databases Historical clinical trials underrepresent minorities and women; publication bias toward positive results Weight data from inclusive trials; incorporate FDA diversity guidelines; flag confidence levels for underrepresented groups

Algorithmic Bias & Proposed Mitigations

Source of Algorithmic Bias Description Affected Use Cases Proposed Mitigation
Label Bias Historical labels reflect existing disparities (e.g., readmission coded differently across hospitals) Predictive Readmission, Treatment Recommendation Standardize labeling criteria; use consensus labeling; conduct inter-annotator agreement checks
Feature Selection Bias Proxy variables (e.g., ZIP code) inadvertently encode race or income All use cases Conduct fairness-aware feature selection; use causal inference to identify proxy features; apply feature ablation testing
Model Complexity Bias Overly complex models may overfit to majority patterns and underperform on minority groups Diagnostic Imaging, Treatment Recommendation Evaluate performance across subgroups; apply regularization; use ensemble methods balancing precision and fairness
Feedback Loop Bias Model outputs influence future data collection, reinforcing existing biases Triage Chatbot, Predictive Readmission Implement exploration strategies; periodic retraining with fresh data; human-in-the-loop review of edge cases
Optimization Objective Bias Optimizing solely for accuracy may sacrifice equity across demographic groups All use cases Use multi-objective optimization including fairness metrics (equalized odds, demographic parity); set minimum performance thresholds per subgroup

Testing, Validation & Monitoring Procedures

Phase Activity Description Frequency
Pre-Deployment Bias Auditing Evaluate model outputs across protected attributes (age, sex, race, income) using fairness metrics Before every release
Pre-Deployment Adversarial Testing Stress-test models with edge cases, out-of-distribution inputs, and adversarial examples Before every release
Pre-Deployment Clinical Validation Validate AI recommendations against expert clinician judgments in blinded studies Initial deployment & major updates
Post-Deployment Performance Monitoring Track accuracy, precision, recall, and fairness metrics in production dashboards Continuous (real-time)
Post-Deployment Drift Detection Monitor for data drift and concept drift; alert when model performance degrades Weekly automated checks
Post-Deployment User Feedback Loops Collect clinician and patient feedback on AI outputs; flag disagreements for review Ongoing
Periodic Review Model Retraining Retrain models with updated, balanced datasets incorporating new demographic data Quarterly
Periodic Review Third-Party Audit Engage independent auditors to review AI systems for bias, safety, and regulatory compliance Annually

Conclusion & Personal Reflections

Difficulties & Questions from This Workshop

One of the biggest challenges I encountered during this material was understanding how to balance competing fairness metrics. For instance, achieving demographic parity in one dimension (e.g., equal prediction rates across racial groups) can sometimes conflict with equalized odds (equal error rates). Deciding which fairness definition to prioritize requires deep domain context and stakeholder input, and there is no one-size-fits-all answer.


Another difficulty was recognizing how deeply embedded bias can be in data that appears objective. Electronic health records, for example, reflect decades of systemic inequities in access to care. Simply training on historical data without intervention perpetuates those inequities. This realization highlighted the importance of proactive data curation and the limitations of purely technical solutions.


A question I still have is: How do smaller organizations with limited resources implement comprehensive bias monitoring? The tools and processes described in literature often assume large teams and budgets. Identifying scalable, practical approaches for smaller companies remains an open challenge that I hope to explore further in my career.
