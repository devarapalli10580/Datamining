AI Use Cases Under Consideration

An AI system that assists physicians by analyzing patient records, lab results, and medical imaging to recommend potential diagnoses and treatment plans. Used in partner hospitals and clinics.

2. Credit Risk Scoring

4. Internal Document Summarization

An internal AI tool that automatically summarizes lengthy reports, meeting notes, and research papers for employees to improve productivity and knowledge sharing.

Explainability Requirements by Use Case

AI Use Case Explainability Required? Level Rationale
Clinical Decision Support Yes – Mandatory High Directly impacts patient health outcomes. Physicians need to understand AI reasoning to make informed medical decisions. Regulatory requirements (FDA, HIPAA) mandate transparency in clinical tools. Errors can result in patient harm or legal liability.
Credit Risk Scoring Yes – Mandatory High Subject to fair lending regulations (ECOA, FCRA). Applicants have a legal right to understand why credit was denied. Must demonstrate absence of discriminatory bias. Regulators require model interpretability for audit purposes.
Customer Service Chatbot Partial – Recommended Medium Lower risk since humans handle escalations. Transparency helps users understand bot limitations. Useful for debugging incorrect responses. Not legally required but improves trust and user satisfaction.
Internal Document Summarization No – Optional Low Used internally for productivity; no external stakeholder impact. Employees can verify summaries against source documents. Low risk of harm from errors. Explainability is nice-to-have but not critical for deployment.

Key Principle: Explainability requirements scale with the potential impact on individuals' rights, health, financial status, and the degree of regulatory oversight applicable to the domain.

Step Description Key Activities
1. Risk Classification Categorize the AI system by impact level (High/Medium/Low) Assess potential harm, regulatory scope, affected populations, reversibility of decisions
2. Stakeholder Identification Identify who needs explanations and at what level of detail Map end users, regulators, internal teams, affected individuals; determine technical vs. non-technical needs
3. Explainability Method Selection Choose appropriate explanation techniques based on model type and audience Select from SHAP, LIME, feature importance, decision trees, counterfactual explanations, natural language summaries
4. Testing & Validation Verify that explanations are accurate, consistent, and comprehensible User testing with target audiences, accuracy verification of explanations, consistency checks across similar inputs
5. Ongoing Monitoring Continuously evaluate explainability as models evolve Regular audits, drift detection, user feedback collection, re-validation after model updates

Documentation to Promote Transparency

AI Development Stage Documentation Artifact Purpose & Contents
Problem Definition & Planning AI Impact Assessment Documents intended use, affected populations, potential risks, explainability requirements, and ethical considerations before development begins.
Data Collection & Preparation Data Card / Datasheet Records data sources, collection methods, known biases, representativeness, preprocessing steps, and limitations of training data.
Model Development Model Card Describes model architecture, training procedures, hyperparameters, performance metrics, intended use cases, known limitations, and fairness evaluations.
Testing & Validation Evaluation Report Documents test results, bias audits, explainability test outcomes, edge case analysis, and comparison against baseline approaches.
Deployment Transparency Notice / User Guide Provides end users with clear information about AI involvement, how decisions are made, how to request explanations, and how to appeal automated decisions.
Post-Deployment Monitoring Audit Log & Monitoring Report Tracks model performance over time, records drift incidents, documents retraining events, logs user feedback, and maintains compliance records.

Guiding Standard: Documentation follows frameworks inspired by Google's Model Cards, Microsoft's Datasheets for Datasets, and the EU AI Act's transparency obligations.

Concluding Reflections: Difficulties & Questions

Challenges Encountered

  • Balancing complexity and clarity: Determining the right level of explanation for different audiences is genuinely difficult. A data scientist and a patient need very different types of explanations from the same system.
  • Explainability vs. performance trade-off: More interpretable models (e.g., decision trees) often sacrifice predictive accuracy compared to black-box models (e.g., deep neural networks). Deciding where this trade-off is acceptable requires careful ethical judgment.
  • Defining "sufficient" transparency: There is no universal standard for what makes an AI system "transparent enough." Different regulators, industries, and stakeholders have varying expectations.

Open Questions

  • How do we measure whether an explanation is truly understood by its intended audience, rather than just provided?
  • As AI models become more complex (e.g., large language models), will current explainability techniques remain adequate?
  • How should companies handle situations where full transparency might expose proprietary algorithms or create security vulnerabilities?
  • What role should industry self-regulation play versus government-mandated transparency requirements?

These reflections represent my personal observations from engaging with the course material on AI explainability and transparency. The intersection of technical capability and ethical responsibility remains one of the most challenging and important aspects of responsible AI development.
